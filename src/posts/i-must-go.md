It barks. I tell it to hush. It doesn’t stop, so I yank the leash. It whimpers, but I don’t mean to hurt it. I just don’t know what else to do. It is vigilant. It wants to protect me. I used to feel protected. But now, I grow anxious. I think it is overactive; I was never taught how to wield it. It barks again, then snarls. My heart rate accelerates as I look to see what has ticked it off. It tugs harder, and I am moved in its direction. Sweat gathers in my palms. I widen my stance. I was told that shortening the leash would give me more control, but I begin to question that wisdom as my rubber soles fight for traction. It has grown ferocious and grotesque. I grew up with it and yet, what I see now is unrecognizable. Old calluses tear and blood trickles down my hand as my grip starts to slip. I lean back and force all the sinew in my arms to hold on. For every good thing in my life, it has been by my side. Oh, it is a horrid-looking thing now. I am ashamed to be seen with it. I am ashamed to be seen struggling with it. I try to let go, but now my vice grip is involuntary. I use one hand to pry open the fingers of the other. I hurt with every second, but I do not stop. As they come off, one by one, it calms down, losing its nightmare-ish figure. It quits struggling. Finally, the leash falls to the floor. I look at it, and it looks at me. I tell it to go, for despite its docile form, all I can see now is its violence. That, and my own bloodied hands. It does not leave, and instead picks up the leash, urging me to take it. But my heart has stopped racing and I feel lighter. Dread evaporates from my flesh. This lightness brings forth memories, flashes of a much younger me. I step back. It whimpers, but if it won’t leave then … I must go. 

I must go.
